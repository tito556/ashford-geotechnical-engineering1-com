Raft and Mat Foundation Design in Ashford: Site-Specific Ground Engineering

Knowledgeable. Thorough. Resourceful.

LEARN MORE

Ashford's expansion as a rail hub and its post-war residential growth placed heavy demand on the underlying Weald Clay and river terrace deposits. Much of the town centre sits on stiff, fissured clay that swells and shrinks with seasonal moisture change, while the Stour floodplain introduces soft alluvium and peat lenses at depth. These conditions mean that isolated footings often cannot deliver the differential settlement control required for medium-rise masonry structures. A raft or mat foundation distributes building loads across the full footprint, bridging local soft spots and reducing angular distortion below 1/500, which is the serviceability threshold for brickwork cracking. The design process follows the limit-state philosophy of BS EN 1997-1:2004, supported by ground investigation data from test pits that map the desiccated crust and CPT soundings that trace the transition into intact clay at 5 to 9 metres below ground level. Ashford's geology demands careful parameter selection, particularly for undrained shear strength, which can drop from over 120 kPa in the weathered zone to 60 kPa where groundwater softens the clay fabric.

In Ashford's Weald Clay, a well-designed raft can limit total settlement to under 40 mm and differential movement to less than 15 mm across a 25-metre building footprint.

Scope of work

The field equipment we deploy for raft foundation characterisation in Ashford typically includes a 20-tonne CPT truck with a 15 cm² cone penetrometer, capable of pushing through dense gravel stringers found in the Hythe Beds at the southern edge of town. Continuous tip resistance and sleeve friction profiles, logged at 2 cm intervals, reveal the depth to competent bearing strata and highlight any trapped sand layers that could drain pore pressure during construction. In parallel, we use a multi-channel seismograph with 24 geophones for MASW surveys to measure shear-wave velocity and derive the small-strain stiffness profile that feeds directly into settlement calculations under BS 8004:2015. For sites near the East Stour, where groundwater sits barely 1.5 m below ground level, the investigation includes standpipe piezometers to track seasonal water table fluctuations that affect buoyancy forces on the raft underside. Laboratory testing on Shelby tube samples from the clay horizon determines the compression index Cc and swelling index Cs, which govern the magnitude of consolidation settlement under long-term loading. The interplay between in-situ penetration data and laboratory-derived stiffness moduli forms the backbone of the numerical model, ensuring the raft thickness and reinforcement layout reflect the ground's actual stress-strain behaviour rather than generic textbook values.

Area-specific notes

A five-storey residential block on Beaver Road encountered a peat-filled palaeochannel that did not appear on the historical borehole records from the 1970s. The initial ground investigation, limited to three window-sample boreholes, missed the feature entirely, and the structural engineer proposed a 400 mm thick raft on the assumption of uniform clay. During excavation, the channel was exposed at the northwest corner, revealing fibrous peat with an organic content above 25 percent. This material exhibits secondary compression that cannot be halted by preloading alone. The raft design had to be revised to incorporate a deepened edge beam and a geogrid-reinforced granular mattress to span the soft zone, transferring load to the competent clay shoulders on either side. The lesson from this Ashford project is that mat foundation performance depends entirely on the spatial resolution of the site investigation. Missing a buried channel or a lens of running sand beneath a raft can convert a cost-effective shallow solution into a long-term serviceability failure, with cracking appearing within the first two years of occupation.

Common questions

What is the typical cost range for a raft foundation design in Ashford?

For a project in Ashford, the geotechnical investigation and structural design of a raft foundation typically ranges from £900 to £2,920, depending on the plan area, number of boreholes and the complexity of the ground model required to satisfy building control.

How does the Weald Clay affect raft foundation performance?

Weald Clay is a stiff, overconsolidated formation with a desiccated crust that can be 2 to 4 metres thick. Its shrink-swell potential means that raft foundations must be placed below the zone of seasonal moisture fluctuation, typically at 1.0 to 1.2 metres depth, and designed to accommodate modest heave pressures if trees are present or have been removed.

When is a raft foundation preferable to strip footings in Ashford?

Rafts become the preferred solution when the allowable bearing pressure for strip footings drops below 75 kPa, when differential settlement between column positions exceeds 20 mm, or when the building footprint covers ground with variable soil thickness. In Ashford's river corridor, rafts also reduce the risk of localised softening under individual footings due to fluctuating groundwater.

What site investigation is needed for a raft foundation design?

A compliant investigation under BS EN 1997-2 normally includes at least one borehole per 200 m² of footprint to a depth of 1.5 times the raft width, supplemented by CPT soundings for continuous profiling and MASW lines for stiffness data. In Ashford, we also install piezometers where the raft base extends below the groundwater table.
