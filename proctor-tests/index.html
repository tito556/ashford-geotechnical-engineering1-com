Proctor Testing in Ashford: Compaction Control for Earthworks and Pavements

Knowledgeable. Thorough. Resourceful.

LEARN MORE

Ashford’s building boom isn’t just concrete and steel—it starts with dirt. Properly compacted fill makes the difference between a stable platform and a settlement claim two winters later. We see a lot of local sites where the natural ground is Weald Clay, and the fill is imported granular material from the Stour valley pits. Getting the moisture–density relationship right on that mix isn’t optional. BS 1377-4 gives the procedure, but the real skill is matching compactive effort to the spec—whether it’s a housing estate in Singleton, a commercial slab off the A28, or a Section 278 road widening. Our CBR testing for road subgrades often runs alongside Proctor curves on the same sample, which saves the contractor a second mobilisation and keeps the programme tight.

A site-specific Proctor curve on Ashford’s Weald Clay can shift optimum moisture by 3–5% compared to a borrowed curve—enough to fail a compaction test.

Scope of work

There’s a real split in Ashford between the heavy clays south of the M20 and the lighter silty gravels near the Stour floodplain. A Proctor curve on clay from a site near Chilmington Green looks completely different from one on terrace gravel out by Wye—peak dry density can jump from 1.72 Mg/m³ to over 2.10 Mg/m³, and optimum moisture content swings the other way. That’s why we never use a generic family curve when we can run the actual test. For sites with variable borrow sources, we pair the Proctor with a sand cone density test to confirm field compaction against the lab target, and with Atterberg limits to flag any clay fraction changes that would shift the whole reference. BS 1377-4 is our baseline, but we also run the heavier modified effort when the spec calls for it—common on highways and deep-fill embankments. One curve, done right, can anchor the whole earthworks QA.

Area-specific notes

The Hythe Beds and Atherfield Clay that underlie much of Ashford don’t compact like uniform sand. They’re sensitive to moisture, and a few percent over optimum can mean the difference between stiff and spongy. We’ve seen pads rejected because the imported fill was compacted wet-of-optimum without anyone checking the actual Proctor curve—the contractor assumed a number, and the nuclear gauge readings looked fine until the first wet week. BS 6031 for earthworks and Eurocode 7 both expect a defensible reference density. On sites with deeper cut-and-fill, we sometimes combine the Proctor with in-situ permeability testing to check whether the compacted layer will also act as a barrier, and with slope stability analysis when the fill forms part of a retained embankment.

Common questions

How much does a Proctor test cost in Ashford?

A standard Proctor (BS Light) on a single sample typically runs between £80 and £160, depending on whether it’s a one-off or part of a larger earthworks testing package. Modified Proctor is at the upper end of that range. If you need multiple curves for different borrow sources, we quote a reduced rate per sample. The price includes the full moisture–density curve, optimum moisture content, and a signed report.
